# OP TTRPG — Origin Stories

> Origin Stories replace classes from the SRD 5.2. They represent what happened to your character — the defining event or circumstance that set them on the path to becoming a hero (or villain). Each Origin Story maps mechanically to an SRD 5.2 class under the hood, but the player-facing identity is purely narrative.
>
> Levels 1–12. All Origin Stories progress across 12 levels.

---

## The Bereaved

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Barbarian*

**"Everyone I loved is gone."**

You lost everything — family, home, purpose. What's left is grief, rage, and the inability to stay down. The pain doesn't make you weaker. It makes you reckless, relentless, and terrifying. You fight like someone with nothing left to lose, because you are.

**Archetype:** The traumatized powerhouse. Wolverine after losing everyone. The Punisher before he found a method. Bruce Banner if the anger never stopped.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Rage, Reckless Attack, Danger Sense, Unarmored Defense, Weapon Mastery, Primal Knowledge, Extra Attack, Brutal Strike, Relentless Rage, Persistent Rage.

---

## The Celebrity

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Bard*

**"Everyone knows my name."**

You're famous — or infamous. Maybe you were a performer, a public figure, a media darling, or a controversial name that never left the headlines. Your power is influence: people listen to you, fight harder around you, and underestimate you at their peril. You have resources, contacts, and a presence that fills a room.

**Archetype:** The charismatic leader. Tony Stark's public persona. Oracle as information broker. A hero whose greatest weapon is that everyone already knows who they are.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Bardic Inspiration (scaling Bardic Die), Spellcasting (reflavored as influence/resources/presence), Jack of All Trades, Expertise, Countercharm, Font of Inspiration, Magical Secrets.

---

## The Chosen

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Cleric*

**"I was given this power. I don't know by what."**

Something chose you. You didn't seek it out — it found you. The power is real: you can heal, protect, and channel forces that shouldn't be possible. But the source? That's the question you can't answer. Maybe it's divine. Maybe it's something older. Maybe it's something that hasn't revealed its true nature yet. You carry the gift and the uncertainty in equal measure.

**Archetype:** The reluctant vessel. Doctor Strange before he understood. A faith healer who doesn't know if their faith is well-placed. Someone touched by a power that may or may not have their best interests at heart.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Spellcasting (reflavored as channeled power), Divine Order, Channel Divinity, Divine Spark, Sear Undead (reflavored), Cleric Subclass (reflavored as the nature of the gift), Divine Intervention.

**Worldbuilding Note:** Divine power in OP is ambiguous. The power is real and measurable. The source is not confirmed. Whether gods, cosmic forces, or something else is behind it remains an open question in the setting. Individual Chosen may believe whatever they want — the system doesn't confirm or deny.

## The Shifter

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Druid*

**"I can become something else."**

You can transform — physically, completely — into animals. Whether this is a mutation, a bond with something primal, or an ability inherited from a bloodline no one understands, the result is the same: your body is not fixed. You are human, and you are also something else, and the line between the two gets blurrier the more you shift.

**Archetype:** Beast Boy. Venom (symbiote as shifting). A mutant whose power is biological transformation. Someone who is losing track of where the human ends and the animal begins.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Wild Shape (reworked in 5.2), Spellcasting (reflavored as primal/instinctual abilities), Druid Subclass (reflavored as the nature of the shifting bond).

---

## The Soldier

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Fighter*

**"I was built for this."**

You were trained — military, paramilitary, private security, underground fighting circuits, or just a lifetime of discipline. You don't have powers. You don't need them. You have skill, experience, and the ability to adapt to any combat situation faster than anyone else in the room. While others rely on abilities they were born with or given, you rely on what you earned.

**Archetype:** Batman (training and discipline over powers). Black Widow. Captain America as a peak-human soldier. The person in the room who doesn't have powers and is still the most dangerous.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Fighting Style, Weapon Mastery, Action Surge, Extra Attack (multiple), Second Wind, Indomitable, Fighter Subclass.

---

## The Disciple

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Monk*

**"I mastered myself first."**

You were trained in a specific tradition — a martial art, a monastic discipline, a philosophical school that taught you to weaponize your own body and mind. Your power comes from within, cultivated through years of practice, meditation, and physical conditioning. You move faster, hit harder, and endure more than any normal human should, and it's all earned.

**Archetype:** Iron Fist. Shang-Chi. A speedster whose speed comes from discipline rather than mutation. Someone whose body operates at a level that borders on supernatural, but it's all technique.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Martial Arts, Focus Points (reflavored as discipline/focus/internal energy), Unarmored Defense, Unarmored Movement, Deflect Attacks, Uncanny Metabolism, Empowered Strikes, Extra Attack, Monk Subclass.

---

## The Oathsworn

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Paladin*

**"I swore an oath and it gave me strength."**

You made a promise — to protect the innocent, to never kill, to uphold justice, to serve something greater than yourself. And somehow, that promise gave you power. The oath is real and binding: as long as you hold to it, you are stronger, more resilient, and capable of things that shouldn't be possible. Break it, and you lose everything.

**Archetype:** Superman's moral code as a literal power source. Captain America's conviction. A hero who is powerful precisely because they will not compromise. The boy scout who could bench press a car.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Lay on Hands (reflavored as protective/restorative touch), Spellcasting, Weapon Mastery, Paladin's Smite (reflavored as conviction-powered strikes), Abjure Foes, Aura of Protection, Restoring Touch, Extra Attack, Paladin Subclass (reflavored as the specific oath made).

**Design Note:** The Oathsworn and The Chosen share the Paladin/Cleric overlap from 5e. The distinction in OP: The Chosen received power from an external, ambiguous source. The Oathsworn generated power from an internal commitment. One is a vessel; the other is a forge.

---

## The Hunter

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Ranger*

**"Someone has to make them pay."**

You were wronged — and now you hunt. Not one specific person (though maybe it started that way), but anyone who does what was done to you. You track, you study, you prepare, and then you strike. You're not reckless. You're methodical. Patient. And you don't stop.

**Archetype:** The Punisher with a plan. John Wick. Hawkeye or Green Arrow — precision and preparation over raw power. A vigilante who hunts wrongdoers with the patience of a predator.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Favored Enemy (reworked in 5.2 — reflavored as studied targets/criminal types), Weapon Mastery, Fighting Style, Spellcasting (reflavored as preparation, traps, tactical advantages), Extra Attack, Relentless Hunter, Precise Hunter, Foe Slayer, Ranger Subclass.

---

## The Scoundrel

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Rogue*

**"Nobody was coming to save me, so I saved myself."**

You grew up on your own — or close enough. No one taught you to fight fair because no one was around to teach you anything. You learned to survive by being smarter, faster, and dirtier than everyone else. You don't have a code. You have instincts. You're not a villain, but you're not exactly a hero either. You're the person who does what needs to be done when the heroes won't.

**Archetype:** Catwoman. Red Hood. Deadpool (minus the fourth wall). The anti-hero who operates in the gray area because the black-and-white heroes can't get the job done.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Sneak Attack, Expertise, Weapon Mastery, Cunning Action, Uncanny Dodge, Cunning Strike, Evasion, Thieves' Cant (reflavored as underworld connections/street code), Devious Mind, Rogue Subclass.

---

## The Mutant

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Sorcerer*

**"I've always been like this."**

You were born with it. You didn't choose your powers, didn't earn them, didn't make a deal for them. They were just... there. Maybe they manifested in childhood. Maybe they erupted during puberty. Maybe you spent years suppressing them before they broke through. The power is part of you — genetic, innate, inseparable from who you are. Learning to control it is the work of a lifetime.

**Archetype:** The X-Men. Storm. Jean Grey. Static Shock. Any hero whose powers are a birthright they never asked for and are still learning to master.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Spellcasting (reflavored as innate mutant abilities), Innate Sorcery, Font of Magic, Sorcery Points, Metamagic (reflavored as pushing/bending/overloading your powers), Sorcery Incarnate, Sorcerer Subclass (reflavored as the nature of the mutation).

---

## The Pactbound

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Warlock*

**"Something offered me power. I said yes."**

You made a deal. Maybe you were desperate. Maybe you were ambitious. Maybe you didn't fully understand what you were agreeing to. But something — an entity, an organization, a force, a presence — offered you power, and you took it. The power is real. The strings attached are also real. You're stronger than you were, but you're not free.

**Archetype:** Ghost Rider. Spawn. Venom (symbiote as pact). A hero who sold something to gain power and is now navigating the consequences.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Pact Magic (reflavored as granted power), Eldritch Invocations (reflavored as manifestations of the pact), Pact Boon, Magical Cunning, Contact Patron, Warlock Subclass (reflavored as the entity/force behind the deal), Eldritch Blast (reflavored as the signature ability granted by the pact).

---

## The Genius

*Mapped from: SRD 5.2 Wizard*

**"I built this with my own mind."**

You don't have powers. You have a brain that operates on a level most people can't comprehend. You built your abilities — literally. Gadgets, suits, weapons, chemical compounds, algorithms, devices. Everything you can do, you engineered. Your "spells" are inventions. Your "spellbook" is a workshop, a lab, or a hard drive full of schematics. You are the smartest person in any room, and that is your superpower.

**Archetype:** Iron Man. Batman (gadget side). Mr. Fantastic. A hero whose power is intellect and preparation — take away the tools and they'll just build new ones.

**Core Mechanics (SRD):** Spellcasting (reflavored as inventions/gadgets/devices), Ritual Adept (reflavored as prep time), Scholar, Spellbook (reflavored as schematics/blueprints/workshop), Arcane Recovery (reflavored as rapid prototyping/field repairs), Memorize Spell, Wizard Subclass (reflavored as area of expertise).

## Multiclassing: A New Origin

In OP, multiclassing isn't just a mechanical decision — it's a narrative one. Your Origin Story is the defining event that made you who you are. Taking a second Origin Story means something *else* happened to you. Something big enough to fundamentally change you again.

A player who wants to multiclass must work with the GM to establish an **Inciting Event** — a specific in-game moment, tied to a level-up, that justifies the new Origin. This isn't a gate to prevent multiclassing; it's an opportunity to make it meaningful. The best Inciting Events emerge naturally from play.

### Examples

- A **Soldier** who watches their team die and can't save them might become a **Bereaved** — the trauma breaks something open, and now the discipline is gone, replaced by raw fury.
- A **Scoundrel** who encounters an entity in a moment of desperation might become a **Pactbound** — they were already willing to bend the rules, and now something offered them real power.
- A **Mutant** who studies their own abilities obsessively might become a **Genius** — the innate power is still there, but now they understand it and can engineer around it.
- A **Chosen** whose mysterious benefactor goes silent might become a **Hunter** — the gift remains, but the purpose has shifted from service to vengeance.
- A **Celebrity** who suffers a public betrayal might become a **Bereaved** — fame couldn't protect them, and now the charm is backed by something darker.

### Rules

- **Narrative requirement.** The player and GM must agree on an Inciting Event before the multiclass happens. This can be planned in advance or emerge from play.
- **Timing.** The Inciting Event occurs at or around a level-up. It doesn't need to happen in the same session, but it should be recent and relevant.
- **No silent multiclassing.** A player cannot simply add a new Origin Story between sessions without narrative justification. The table should know what happened and why.
- **Standard SRD 5.2 multiclassing prerequisites and rules apply mechanically.** The narrative layer is additional, not a replacement for the mechanical requirements.

### Design Note

This system turns multiclassing from a min-max optimization choice into a character development moment. The question isn't "what's the best dip for my build?" — it's "what happened to my character that changed them?" Some of the best stories in superhero fiction come from exactly this kind of transformation: the hero who loses their way, gains new power, or is fundamentally altered by events. OP should reward that.

## Design Notes

- **Origin Stories are narrative-first, mechanical-second.** The name and concept describe what happened to the character. The SRD class underneath provides the mechanical chassis. Players engage with the story; the system handles the math.
- **Under the hood, each Origin Story maps 1:1 to an SRD 5.2 class.** This means all SRD class features, subclass structures, and progression tables can be used as the mechanical foundation, then reflavored for the OP setting.
- **Spellcasting is reflavored per Origin Story.** The Chosen channels an unknown power. The Mutant expresses innate abilities. The Genius uses inventions. The Pactbound draws on granted power. The mechanical system (spell slots, spell lists, components) remains; the fiction changes.
- **Subclasses become sub-origins or story branches.** Each Origin Story will eventually have subclass equivalents that represent different expressions of the same story. A Bereaved who channels cold fury is different from one who channels explosive rage — same Origin, different path.
- **The "holy trinity" of physical Origin Stories** — Soldier, Disciple, Oathsworn — are distinguished by motivation, not capability. The Soldier fights because it's their profession. The Disciple fights because of tradition. The Oathsworn fights because of principle. All three are formidable in combat; the difference is why they're in the fight.
- **The Oathsworn/Chosen distinction** mirrors the Paladin/Cleric split: internal conviction (Oathsworn) vs. external bestowal (Chosen). One forges their own power through commitment; the other receives it from an ambiguous source.
- **Ambiguous divinity** is a deliberate setting choice. The Chosen's power is real. The source is unconfirmed. This allows individual players and GMs to decide whether gods exist in their version of the OP world without the system taking a position.
